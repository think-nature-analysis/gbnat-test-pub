Key Features

Comprehensive datasets covering both terrestrial and marine realms:

GBNAT provides quantitative information on biodiversity and ecosystem for any location worldwide. Both terrestrial and marine environments are covered in our datasets, which include biodiversity importance and intactness, deforestation, human footprint, and waterrelated risks (flood/drought).

Your input is only the location of your activities:

By providing the location of your operations, GBNAT delivers statistics to help assess the biodiversity impact and dependence of your business in an evidence-based, quantitative manner.

Enabling you to nature-related information disclosure and strategy building:

You can use GBNAT to identify priority areas for assessing biodiversity risks, analyze the impact of commodity production or mining, and determine areas where nature restoration will have the greatest positive impact. The data is ready-to-use and applicable in the Locate phase of TNFD.

What is available on GBNAT?

GBNAT can provide biodiversity reports based on our spatial datasets for any given locations of terrestrial and marine realm. GBNAT's assessment includes not only the biodiversity itself, but ecosystem conditions including deforestation rate, human footprint increase, and water related indicators (water quality, flood risk, and drought risk). Data and methodologies behind GBNAT

GBNAT is based on data and methodological innovations that address issues such as insufficiency in resolution and comprehensiveness around the use of biodiversity data in business. For more information, see the following document.

Here are the spatial data layers behind GBNAT Metrics 1/8
Biodiversity Importance (Marine)

An indicator of the importance of site's biodiversity in terms of extinction risk reduction, calculated using the species composition of +30,000 marine species, ranging from O to1, with higher values indicating greater importance. Based on the distribution and rarity of each species, sites are ranked to have the least species loss. It is a direct indicator of priority areas that should be conserved.

Here are the spatial data layers behind GBNAT 2/8
Biodiversity Intanctness

Habitat maps and natural forest distribution data were used to compute the distribution of various natural habitats and artificial environments. Ecosystem integrity was spatially layered by multiplying the obtained spatial layers by a coefficient (MSA value that is 1 for natural conditions and less than 1 if modified: CISL (2020) values were used).

Here are the spatial data layers behind GBNAT 3/8
Ecoregion

Classification of Ecoregions.

Here are the spatial data layers behind GBNAT 4/8
Forest area change

Using satellite image (Landsat) data, changes in globalforests (vegetation with a height greater than 5 m) were modelled and projected ona 1arcsec grid; the difference between the percent forest area in 2000 and the percent in 2020 was calculated.

Here are the spatial data layers behind GBNAT 5/8
Human Footprint Increase

An index that integrates a person's pressure on the environment and scores it between 0 and 50. The increase in this value from 2000 to 2019. Geographic data on building construction, population, power infrastructure, farmland, roads, and canals were combined into a single footprint score.

Here are the spatial data layers behind GBNAT 6/8
Water Quality Risk

Biochemical oxygen demand (BOD) represents the quantity of oxygen necessary for the microbial metabolism of organic substances present in water; a value of 2 or less indicates high quality water, such as a mountain stream. Using a global comprehensive dataset of water quality, we used Random Forest to estimate the linkages to environmental factors. Using this learning model, BOD values were projected globally.

Here are the spatial data layers behind GBNAT 7/8
Flood Frequency

Created by counting the number of times a flood affected the area between 1985 and 2021.

Here are the spatial data layers behind GBNAT 8/8
Drought Possibility

AWARE index: an indicator of how little water is available; a value of 1 indicates that there is as much available water as the global average, while a value of 10 indicates that there is only 1/10th of the global average.
